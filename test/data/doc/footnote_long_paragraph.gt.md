## Global Energy Consumption Trends

Over the past two decades, global energy consumption has undergone a fundamental transformation. The rapid industrialisation of emerging economies, combined with population growth and increasing urbanisation, has driven total primary energy demand to record highs. At the same time, the accelerating deployment of renewable energy technologies such as solar photovoltaics and onshore wind has begun to reshape the generation mix in many regions. Policy frameworks including carbon pricing mechanisms, renewable portfolio standards and national net-zero commitments have further catalysed investment in low-carbon infrastructure. Despite these structural shifts, fossil fuels continue to account for the majority of global energy supply, underscoring the scale of the transition required. The data presented in Table 1 summarises consumption by fuel type across the five largest economies for the reference year, highlighting both the absolute magnitudes involved and the relative share of each source within the national energy mix.

Table 1: Primary energy consumption by fuel type (EJ), reference year.

| Economy   |   Fossil Fuels |   Renewables |   Nuclear |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|-----------|
| USA       |           76.3 |          9.1 |       8.0 |
| China     |          117.8 |         14.2 |       3.9 |
| India     |           31.5 |          5.3 |       0.4 |
| Russia    |           29.1 |          1.1 |       2.7 |
| Japan     |           14.6 |          2.8 |       0.6 |

[^1]

[^1]: Figures are expressed in exajoules (EJ) and sourced from the IEA World Energy Balances, 2023 edition.

[^2]

[^2]: Renewables include solar, wind, hydro, and bioenergy; nuclear is listed separately.

These figures confirm that while the share of renewables is growing, the absolute volume of fossil fuel consumption remains substantial across all five economies.
